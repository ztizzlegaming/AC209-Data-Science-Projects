Trump's Tweets

The importance and impact of emotions in American politics in the digital media age.

Overview

This project uses President Donal Trump's Twitter data and the measurable emotionality of his tweets to predict the number of 'Favorites' a tweet will receive and the daily change in followers.

Problem Statement

There is no doubt that social networks have experienced phenomenal growth in the last 20 years. Facebook, Twitter, Instagram and many others have been the protagonists of one of the biggest technological revolutions. These platforms are used in everyday situations, from coordinating a dinner with friends, to even doing world politics. There are many examples that shows this diversity of uses and impacts. Elon Musk's tweeting his future plans for their companies that generates an instant change on the stocks, or Donald Trump's tweets declaring his support to Venezuela's self-proclaimed President, Juan Guaido. In this context, it is of special interest to research how very influential public figures, such as President Donald Trump, frame their tweets, which emotions they display in them and what impact this creates on the world around them.


In this project we first determined the emotional load of President Donald Trump's tweets, measured as subjectivity and polarization, and use this to subsequently predict the number of "favorites" an individual tweet will get, and how it impacts the daily change in followers.

Data Description and Challenges

Despite the three possible ways to get data on Trump’s Twitter activity (Twitter API, Scraping, Trump’s Tweets Archive), we decided to use the Trump's Tweets Archive ('Archive') because it contained all the relevant information we needed for our analysis including most of the tweets that were deleted later on, the 'Archive' dataset was already tested, we can surf the web page to look for specific tweets in case we encountered any errors or have any doubt. The visualization and filter system are really helpful for this task. Also, as we are analyzing around 45k observations, it is still a manageable number to look them in Excel or other data visualization software, it is faster to implement and, if, for any reason, we would like to include more data for our analysis, we can still use Twitter's API. Further details, see [1] and [2].

We export every tweet and retweet made by Trump from 2012 to November 14th. We include the following variables from every observation:

    1. source (object): From which platform was the tweet tweeted.
    2. text(object): The tweet's text.
    3. created_at (dtetime64): datetime when the tweet was tweeted.
    4. retweet_count (int64): Number of retweets the tweet has.
    5. favorite_count (int64): Number of 'Favorites' the tweet received. Only valid for original tweets (no retweeted tweets).
    6. is_retweet (bool): True if the tweet is a retweeted tweet, False otherwise.
    7. id_str (int64): Tweet's unique ID.

Before importing the dataset to the jupyter notebook we did a fast check on Microsoft Excel. We encountered one thing that needed our attention: 53 observations didn't have data in id_str. To solve this, we filtered the 53 observations and realized that all of them had the id_str in the is_retweet column. We moved the data to the correct column and analyzed whether the observations were retweeted tweets or original tweets. As all of them had 0 in retweet_count AND all their texts started with "RT @", thus we set is_retweet to “True” for all 53 observations. In addition, we converted is_retweet to boolean instead of string values.

Sentiment Analysis of Tweets

Introduction to sentiment analysis with TextBlob (click to read)

Once we had the twitter data, we wanted to perform sentiment analysis on the text of the tweets and retweets. Sentiment analysis is a form of natural language processing that in our case determines two numbers, the polarity and subjectivity. Polarity refers to the orientation of the sentiment of the text, i.e. whether the text is positive, neutral, or negative. Subjectivity refers to how much the text is influenced by personal feelings and emotions. A few examples would be:

    • "Miranda went to the store" → neutral polarity, low sentiment;
    • "Apple computers are the best" → positive polarity, medium sentiment;
    • "I hate it when I get a bad grade" → negative polarity, high sentiment.

We were recommended a few Python packages to perform sentiment analysis by our TF Robbert. The ones we looked at were TextBlob, spaCy, and nltk. spaCy has a lot of functionality, but after a bit of research we realized that it cannot directly do sentiment analysis, so we decided not to use it. nltk has a lot of functionality and could do sentiment analysis, but was a bit more complicated than the other two. TextBlob was very simple and easy to use and did perform sentiment analysis, so we decided to use it. In addition, TextBlob uses nltk in the background to implement a lot of its functionality. We may use spaCy or nltk in the future if we find that they have some functionality that TextBlob does not.

In performing sentiment analysis, TextBlob calculates a number between -1 and 1 for polarity, where -1 is very negative and 1 being very positive, and a number between 0 and 1 for subjectivity, where 0 is not subjective and 1 is very subjective. These numbers are calculated by averaging the polarity and subjectivity scores for each individual word in the text, along with a bit more complex math for negation or the intensity of a word. A more in-depth description of how these numbers are calculated is given here. Again, below we look at the same examples from above.


from textblob import TextBlob
b1 = TextBlob('Miranda went to the store')b1.sentimentSentiment(polarity=0.0, subjectivity=0.0)
b2 = TextBlob('Apple computers are the best')b2.sentimentSentiment(polarity=1.0, subjectivity=0.3)
b3 = TextBlob('I hate it when I get a bad grade')b3.sentimentSentiment(polarity=-0.75, subjectivity=0.7833333333333333)


Sentiment analysis using TextBlob is done by creating a TextBlob object, passing in the text of the tweet, and we getting the polarity and subjectivity values with blob.sentiment. We then store these and save the new data to a file on the disk, as reading in the data from disk is much faster than going through each tweet and generating the sentiment values. There is no randomness in generating the polarity and singularity values, so we only have to do it once then save it. We apply this to all of Donald Trump's tweets since his election, November 9, 2016, and get numeric values of polarity and subjectivity for each tweet.

At this point we have not found any significant limitations of TextBlob. You can do simple operations like splitting the text into individual words or sentences, or more complex operations like sentiment analysis, part-of-speech tagging, translations, conversion to n-grams, and more. In the future we may do something like look at the distribution of the different parts-of-speech used in Trump's tweets, which could be done easily using this package. However, if we do find a limitation as we are using this package, something more powerful like nltk might be necessary.

4. The marginal contribution of including Polarity and Subjectivity

One of the key elements of this project is to predict the impact of measurable emotions in tweets on the two key 'social approval indicators' Favorites and Followers. Given that we found that we cannot predict 'Favorites' with the predictors at hand, while we can fairly good predict the daily change in Followers, we are highly interested in seeing which effect the inclusion of the derived variables Polarity and Subjectivity had. In order to do this, we compare the models' ability to do their specific predicting task with and without the two variables.

Contribution of Polarity and Subjectivity to the models' prediction abilities

Contribution to models predicting 'Favorites' counts

  • In predicting favorites-counts, sentiment does not play much of a role. There is almost no difference between RMSE and R² when including or excluding polarity and subjectivity scores.
  • Our best model is XGB, but the difference is tiny between including or excluding sentiment scores.
  • The largest difference is in the FNN model, but the difference is still marginal.


Contribution to models predicting 'change in followers per day'

  • In predicting percentage_change_followers_next_day, the sentiment scores played a much bigger role, which we can see by the difference in RMSE and R².
  • The gain wasn't huge, but there was a significant gain by including the polarity and subjectivity scores for the tweets.
  • The models predicting change in followers performed much better in general than predicting favorites.


5. Conclusions from the prediction models

Conclusions from prediction task [1] 'Favorites'

  • None of the three models did predict the tweets' favorite_count effectively. All three got high levels of RMSE, and low R² score (below 0.1), which means that all three models are not predicting much.
  • The eXtreme gradient boosted (xgb) model does a slightly better job than the linear baseline model, while the FNN performs worse. Given the very similar (low) scores, choosing one model over another ist not helping much with regards to the predicting task at hand.
  • Removing polarity and subjectivity did not have too much of an effect and the performances of the three models stay similarly low. It is not very surprising that models that were already performing poorly, do not get much worse when two variables are removed. At least it shows that the two derived variables have not too much explanatory power regarding the number of favorites a tweet receives. The biggest change between the models with the two variables and without was detected in the FNN model, but the difference (in terms of R²) is only about 0.01.
  • In conclusion, one interpretation of these results is that we could be right regarding our initial hypothesis that hitting the 'favorites' button is not necessarily triggered by strong emotional reactions to the content of the tweet (i.e. contrary to retweeting).

Conclusions from prediction task [2] 'Change in followers per day'

  • The three models were performing much better in predicting the percentage change in Trump's followers per day than they did in predicting the favorites a tweet would get.
  • The range of R² scores observed in Task 2 was much higher than the one in Task 1.
  • XGB model has the best accuracy results among the three models obtaining an RMSE score of 0.2, and an R² score of 0.41. Again, the FNN Model behaves worse than the baseline model, which tells us that a NN is perhaps not the best algorithm design for this prediction task. These results are consistent with what we found in the models of Task 1.
  • Unlike for the models' performance in Task1, removing polarity and subjectivity from the models for prediction task 2 did have a distinguishable effect on the performance of the models. This is likely because the polarity and subjectivity provided very valuable information regarding the change in followers per day. We aggregated the variables to day-level by taking the mean, min, and max values for polarity and subjectivity for the day. Those six variables give us a pretty significant boost in performance over not including them. The biggest difference is again in the FFN model, of about 0.06 to the R². A difference like this is not groundbreaking, but it is noticeably significant and shows that including measurable proxies for the emotionality of the tweets is significantly helping when the task at hand is predicting the direct change in followers over the next day.

Impact and Final Reflection

The results we have obtained show that when data on the emotional load of tweets is used, machine learning models are still not able to predict the number of 'Favorites' a tweet will receive but are significantly better in predicting the subsequent change in fowllowers over the next 24h. This result is indicating that there is an association between the emotional load within the tweets measured as polarity and subjectivity, and the daily change in followers, while there is potentially no strong association between those two measures of emotional load and the number of 'Favorites'.

The impact of this association can have important consequences on the management of social networks for political and social purposes. In an increasingly polarized context, we need further research as the two proxies, polarity and subjectivity, with which we measure the emotional load of the tweets could be improved to make them contain more accorate information on the sentiment of the tweets. In addition, as this project's aim was prediciton of 'Favorites' and change in followers per day not inference in terms of investigating factors being associated with 'Favorites' and followers not to speak of factors causually influencing them, further research would be needed to investigate drivers behind the two responses. Given the current data generation process, the latter aim of making causal inferences is currently impossible but could be feasible with the right experimentational setup (which could - at least theoretically and highly dependent on the concrete research question - be feasible on platforms like Twitter). In conclusion, it would be of great interest to continue this project on determining the importance and measuring the impact of emotions in American politics in the digital media age with a special emphasis on inference (and potentially even causal inference).

Additional Limitations and further Recommendations

In addition to the limitations mentioned in the previous paragraph 'Impact and Final Reflection', due to the scope of the project, some interesting findings where not further researched. Ideas for further/future projects include:

    • It would be highly interesting pursuing the questions of whether it is actually President Trump himself or his staff who are writing tweets (and whether this makes a difference in the emotional nature of the tweets measured in polarity and subjectivity aka does the author of the tweets make a significant difference regarding polarization and subjectivity patterns)?
    • In addition, it would be interesting to analyze the change in retweeting behavior over the past two years and whether we see significant patterns there (e.g., the increase in his twitter activity could mostly be due to him retweeting more instead of actually writing way more 'new' tweets per day).
    • Third, for the project at hand, we decided to limit the scope of this project to only tweets made by Trump since his election in 2016. It would also be very interesting to compare the pre-election period to the post-election period.
    • Slightly more complex but interesting from a time series point of view: Is there a pattern of emotion in subsequent tweets aka how long lasts a more extreme emotional outburst on average; or framed differently: how many tweets are written given a certain more extreme emotional outburst measured by the polarization and subjectivity of the tweets?
    • In addition, multiple analyses could be possible. A recent New York Times article and ongoing work from the New York Times show how valuable Trump's Twitter data is and how much has changed since the end of 2016. See [4] for further links on the topic.

      3. created_at(dtetime64): datetime when he tweeted the tweet.
      4. retweet_count(int64): amount of retweets the tweet has.
      5. favorite_count(int64): amount of favs the tweet has. Only valid for original tweets (no retweeted tweets by Trump).
      6. is_retweet (bool): True if the tweet is retweeted tweet, False otherwise.
      7. id_str (int64): Tweet's unique ID.
